Game Theory · Mechanism Design · Coordination

Trust is profitable only in the right game.
We are playing the wrong one.

Cooperation fails at scale not because players defect morally, but because the coordination environment violates the four architectural conditions under which reciprocity is stable.
Condition for stable cooperation
Structural violation today
Architectural repair
01 / Iteration
Shadow of the future
Interactions repeat often enough that defection is costly
Collapsed horizon
One-shot interactions
Gig workers, drivers, strangers — you never meet the same counterparty twice
Fix
Persistent trace
Cryptographically verifiable history that travels across platforms and time
02 / Identifiability
Knowing whom to repay
Players can distinguish partners; reciprocity needs an addressable subject
Siloed identity
Reputation locked in platforms
LinkedIn ≠ Upwork ≠ Uber ≠ academia. No cross-border reputational trade
Fix
Portable identity
Decentralized identifiers that live above platforms, not inside them
03 / Memory
Persistent history
Without memory, reciprocity degenerates into random play
Forced amnesia
Exit = reset
Leave a platform, lose your history. Amnesia as retention mechanism
Fix
User-owned causal graph
The history of actions and consequences belongs to the participant, not the provider
04 / Reciprocity
Symmetric sanction
No player can unilaterally rewrite the rules mid-game
Sanction asymmetry
Calvinball with a valuation
Platforms change the rules. Users absorb the change. One rulebook, one writer
Fix
Bounded governance + fork rights
Mathematical caps on influence (e.g. quadratic voting) plus architecturally cheap exit
P(cooperation) ≈ (δ · ID · M · R)ⁿ  ·  multiplicative fragility
From moral appeal → to mechanism design
